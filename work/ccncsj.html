Chinese-Canadian National Council for Social Justice (CCNC-SJ)

About the project

Redesigning the website for the Chinese Canadian National Council for Social Justice, which promotes advocacy and tools for anti-racism driven by COVID-19.

The Challenge

CCNC wanted to improve the overall experience and design of their Wordpress website. They had many conferences, panels, tools, and campaigns lined up and they felt a large gap between them and their users.

Why this is happening

  • Inaccessible: too many photos, lots of small text
  • Inconsistent: Sections not organized in a way that made sense
  • Performance: slow from photos that had little to no context and lots of plugins that weren't improving the site experience
  • Design: Visual language was not cohesive

The Solution

We gave the website a full visual overhaul that made it easy to ingest plenty of new information for their users (young kids, mature adults-seniors). Ideally the site would be easy to navigate, easy to read on all devices, and visually easy to comprehrend between both age groups.

How it was solved

  • Navigation: Reorganized the entire site and each section
  • Simplicity: Only photos that have context with information, removed repetitive sections and multiple CTAs
  • Impoving accessbility: Improved legibility, contrast, and reduced colour palette choice

User Stories

Practical approach

As a victim of racism, I want to know how to respond to racism so people know it’s unacceptable

Emotional approach

As a supporter of Asian-pride & belonging I want to fulfill my pride & my need to belong so I don’t have to compromise my identity

Informational approach

As someone with an open mind I want to stay informed on the main issues so I can help myself and others be better

Site Maps

Reorganizing the entire site was instrumental to improving its usability. CCNC has so much important information for such a wide audience, this first step was foundational. We removed several sections and shuffled them where they would make more sense for the users. I spent a few days speaking with their team about the priorities and goals for each section which, in turn, resulted in a more cohesive and concise site structure.

Old site map

  • Anchored sections that would make more sense as pages (media)
  • Sections appearing in multiple places, with different positioning (Who We Are, FaceRae, Stop the Spread)
  • Sections not making sense under their category (History of Resilience within Who We Are)

New site map

  • Moved sections under categories that provided more context
  • No anchored sections
  • Reduced page bloat by removing unnecessary subsections (ie. Who We Are, Campaigns)
  • Added a resource section for citing links and information
  • Updated the subsections under Campaigns (ie. FaceRace’s About page was a subsection)

Wireframing

I worked around one of the director’s biggest requests for the site: don’t make the change look too drastic. So, I worked around the already existing layout structure and columns so the CCNC team doesn’t feel like they have to relearn how to use their website. For the most part, information was being shuffled.
